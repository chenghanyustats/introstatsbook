6  Definition of Probability

6.1 Language of Uncertainty

Why Study Probability

  • We live in a world full of chances and uncertainty, and probability is the language of uncertainty!
  • Probability is the language of statistical inference and prediction.
  • Below are a few real world examples of where probability plays a role.

Why Probability Before Statistics?

  • For probability, we know the process generating the data and are interested in properties of observations.
  • For statistics, we observe the data (sample) and are interested in determining what process is generating the data (population).

6.2 Interpretation of Probability

Relative Frequency

  • The probability that some outcome of a process will be obtained is interpreted as the relative frequency with which that outcome would be obtained if the process were repeated a large number of times independently under similar conditions.
  • Below is an example depicting the relative frequency of flipping a coin and getting heads or tails.
      Frequency Relative Frequency
Heads         5                0.5
Tails         5                0.5
Total        10                1.0
---------------------
      Frequency Relative Frequency
Heads       467              0.467
Tails       533              0.533
Total      1000              1.000
---------------------
  • If we flip the coin 10 times, the probability of obtaining a head is 50%.
  • If we flip it 1000 times, the probability is 46.7%.

Classical Approach

  • For the classical approach, the probability is based on the concept of equally likely outcomes.
  • If the outcome of some process must be one of \(n\) different outcomes, the probability of each outcome is \(1/n\).
  • Example:
    • If you toss a fair coin (2 outcomes) ü™ô, the probability of each is 1/2.
    • If you roll a well-balanced die (6 outcomes) üé≤, the probability of each is 1/6.
    • If you draw one from a deck of cards (52 outcomes) üÉè, the probability of each is 1/52.
